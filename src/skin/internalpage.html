What are Web Trackers?

A brief explanation of what trackers are and how they operate



Web trackers are technologies embedded into websites usually invisible to the user that collect data about your online activity. They are typically pieces of code (like JavaScript, cookies, or tracking pixels) loaded from third-party servers.

These tools monitor and record:

  • Pages you visit
  • How long you stay on each page
  • What you click or scroll through
  • Your IP address, browser type, and device specs
  • Sometimes geolocation, email, or login IDs

In short, they build a behavioral profile based on your digital actions‚Äîusually without your explicit consent.

Source: Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), "Behind the One-Way Mirror"

Why they track you

A summary of why tracking is used and why data is collected



Trackers exist because user data is extremely valuable. Here's why they do it:

a. Behavioral Advertising

Most tracking is driven by the advertising industry. Advertisers want to understand:

  • Your interests and habits
  • What you're likely to click or buy
  • When you're most active online

Using this, they deliver targeted ads, increasing the chance you'll convert into a customer.

Citation: Turow, J. (2012). The Daily You: How the New Advertising Industry Is Defining Your Identity and Your Worth

b. Analytics and Optimization

Websites use trackers to measure:

  • How many people visit
  • Which pages perform best
  • How users interact with content

This is often done via tools like Google Analytics, though it comes at the cost of sharing your data with third parties.

c. Data Brokerage

Some trackers collect your data not just for the site you're on, but to resell it to data brokers. These companies aggregate data from many sources to build extremely detailed consumer profiles.

Source: Mozilla Foundation, "You Are Not the Product. You Are the Lab Rat."

Common types of trackers

Advertising Analytics Cookies


There are multiple methods used to track users:

A. Cookies

Especially third-party cookies, which track you across multiple sites. For example, a cookie from doubleclick.net can follow you from news.com to shop.com.

  • Set by: Advertisers, analytics providers
  • Used for: Long-term user recognition
  • Persistence: Can last months or even years
Source: Cookiepedia

B. Tracking Pixels / Beacons

Tiny, invisible images (often 1x1 pixel) embedded in a site or email. When loaded, they notify the tracker that you've viewed a specific page or email.

Example URL: https://tracker.example.com/pixel.gif?uid=1234

C. JavaScript-based Trackers

Scripts that collect in-depth data from your device or browser, like clicks, scrolls, screen size, and even typing behavior.

Common source: Google Tag Manager, Hotjar, Facebook Pixel

D. Device Fingerprinting

An advanced method that creates a unique ID based on your browser and hardware configuration‚Äîno cookies required.

Fingerprinting vectors:
  • Screen resolution
  • Browser version
  • Installed fonts
  • WebGL rendering
  • Audio context fingerprint
Tool example: FingerprintJS
Technical resource: EFF's Panopticlick Project

E. CNAME Cloaking

A deceptive practice where third-party trackers are masked to appear as first-party resources. This allows them to bypass browser restrictions.

Example: analytics.site.com actually resolves to track.vendor.com
Source: Mozilla Developer Docs on CNAME Cloaking

How to protect Yourself

Tips and tools for protecting your privacy online



It's not easy to completely avoid tracking, but you can greatly reduce your exposure with a few tools and habits.

A. Use Privacy-Focused Browsers

  • Brave: Blocks most trackers and ads by default
  • Firefox: Has strong Enhanced Tracking Protection
  • Tor Browser: Best for anonymity (but can be slower)
Firefox Guide ‚Üí

B. Install Tracker-Blocking Extensions

uBlock Origin Privacy Badger (by EFF) DuckDuckGo Privacy Essentials

These tools detect and block known tracker domains in real-time.

C. Use DNS-Based Blockers

  • NextDNS or AdGuard DNS can block trackers at the network level
  • Pi-hole is a local DNS sinkhole for advanced users
NextDNS ‚Üí Pi-hole Docs ‚Üí

D. Regularly Clear Cookies and Local Storage

Especially important if you're using a browser that allows trackers by default.

  • Also clear IndexedDB and LocalStorage, which trackers use as backups

E. Disable JavaScript (if possible)

Many trackers rely on JavaScript. Disabling it can break trackers‚Äîbut also many websites.

Use extensions like NoScript if you need fine-grained control.

F. Limit Social Media Widgets

Avoid clicking embedded Like or Share buttons, which often track you even if you don't interact with them. These can load scripts from:

facebook.net twitter.com linkedin.com

G. Use a VPN

While a VPN doesn't stop tracking directly, it obscures your IP address and location, making fingerprinting harder.

Reminder: VPNs don't stop JavaScript trackers, cookies, or fingerprinting‚Äîthey only mask IP-level identifiers.
